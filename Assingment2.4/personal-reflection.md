# Sprint 1 Personal Reflection

## Key Learning

The most important thing I learned is that estimation is not just about how long something takes to build. It is about understanding everything the story needs before it can be called done. Story 9 was estimated at 5 points based on what was visible in the user story description. What nobody noticed was that the Technical Notes section mentioned an audit trail logging requirement. That one requirement added a full extra day of backend work that changed everything. From now on I will read the Technical Notes and the Definition of Done word for word before agreeing to any point value because the visible feature is never the whole picture.

## Assumptions That Failed

The assumption that failed the most was believing that an external dependency would be resolved quickly just because it seemed like a small request. The team assumed IT would send the email credentials within a day or two. They did not arrive for the entire six day sprint. This affected Story 1 because the email confirmation could not be tested with the real service. The lesson here is that anything that depends on another team or department cannot be assumed. It has to be confirmed before the sprint starts. If it is not confirmed then it must be listed as a risk in the planning record with a backup plan ready from Day 1.

## Behavioural Change

In Sprint 2 I would change the way I handle early warning signs during the standup. In Sprint 1 there was a moment on Day 3 where Story 9 felt larger than its estimate suggested but nothing was said because it was not yet a confirmed problem. By the time it was raised on Day 5 the sprint had already lost two days of time to respond. The change is simple. If something feels wrong I will say it in that day's standup. The standup is not only for reporting what is done. It is also the team's early warning system and it only works if people speak up about uncertainty and not just about certainty.

## Professional Growth

The skill I need to build is structured estimation. Right now I tend to read a user story at a high level and make a judgment call on the points. A more disciplined approach would be to mentally list all the individual pieces of work before agreeing to a number. For Story 9 that would have meant thinking through the conflict detection query, the resolution screen, the email notification to the cancelled user, and the audit trail logging separately. Doing that kind of mental breakdown before estimating would have shown the team that 5 points was not enough. This is exactly how Planning Poker is supposed to work and developing this habit will make my estimates more reliable and reduce surprises during the sprint.
